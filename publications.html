Master Thesis

Epistemology of the Emergent Subject: Models of Collaboration in the Event Horizon Telescope Scientific Enterprise

2025 · Università degli Studi di Milano

Abstract

The field of Social epistemology addresses multiple questions concerning the nature of knowledge as a product of a human collective. Among these, the question concerning the summative or non-summative nature of a group of epistemic agents represents a significant role. In this context, we note a convergence of the literature on three models for the conceptualization of the collective, namely Joint Commitment, Distributed Cognition, and Extended Mind, each offering different answers to the question of summativity.

Although the first two models are cited and used extensively in literature, we show that they face significant limitations in adequately capturing the complexity of scientific research collaborations. Specifically, Joint Commitment assumes and imposes a hardly verifiable uniform set of shared beliefs on epistemic agents. On the other hand, Distributed Cognition reduces the collective to a mere aggregate of functional connections, without providing tools for conceptualizing the emergence of a collective entity. The Extended Mind model, although it offers a liberal and radical functionalist approach, may offer a promising alternative for framing the emergence of the collective through the concept of a coupled system, which manifests as a shared cognitive space characterized by the manipulation by both intentional and non-intentional epistemic agents of the internal and external representations that populate it.

To illustrate this, the scientific collaboration behind the Event Horizon Telescope is reconstructed as a concrete example for comparing the various models. The intricate path toward obtaining the first image of M87, characterized by complex phases of technical and bureaucratic organization and by the use of algorithms and black-hole imaging techniques to produce an adequate, bias-free image, exemplifies a common cognitive space. Within this space, collective transformation of shared internal and external representations occurred through precise mechanisms of trust.

In conclusion, we suggest, as EHT does, that other research collectives may also meet the requirements to establish a shared cognitive space. This consideration raises, among other lines of inquiry, interesting normative questions about the criteria for the emergence of such a space and the mechanisms for attributing the status of shared representation.
