Master Thesis

Epistemology of the Emergent Subject: Models of Collaboration in the Event Horizon Telescope Scientific Enterprise

2025 · Università degli Studi di Milano

Abstract

The field of Social epistemology addresses multiple questions concerning the nature of knowledge as a product of a human collective. Among these, the question concerning the summative or non-summative nature of a group of epistemic agents represents a significant role. In this context, we note a convergence of the literature on three models for the conceptualization of the collective, namely Joint Commitment, Distributed Cognition, and Extended Mind, each offering different answers to the question of summativity.

Although the first two models are cited and used extensively in literature, we show that they face significant limitations in adequately capturing the complexity of scientific research collaborations. Specifically, Joint Commitment assumes and imposes a hardly verifiable uniform set of shared beliefs on epistemic agents. On the other hand, Distributed Cognition reduces the collective to a mere aggregate of functional connections, without providing tools for conceptualizing the emergence of a collective entity. The Extended Mind model, although it offers a liberal and radical functionalist approach, may offer a promising alternative for framing the emergence of the collective through the concept of a coupled system, which manifests as a shared cognitive space characterized by the manipulation by both intentional and non-intentional epistemic agents of the internal and external representations that populate it.

To illustrate this, the scientific collaboration behind the Event Horizon Telescope is reconstructed as a concrete example for comparing the various models. The intricate path toward obtaining the first image of M87, characterized by complex phases of technical and bureaucratic organization and by the use of algorithms and black-hole imaging techniques to produce an adequate, bias-free image, exemplifies a common cognitive space. Within this space, collective transformation of shared internal and external representations occurred through precise mechanisms of trust.

In conclusion, we suggest, as EHT does, that other research collectives may also meet the requirements to establish a shared cognitive space. This consideration raises, among other lines of inquiry, interesting normative questions about the criteria for the emergence of such a space and the mechanisms for attributing the status of shared representation.

Bachelor Thesis

The Epistemology of Ernst Mach and Its Convergences with Pragmatism

2023 · Università degli Studi di Milano

Abstract

This bachelor’s thesis investigates the epistemology of Ernst Mach with the aim of assessing the legitimacy of its convergence with classical pragmatism. While Mach is traditionally framed within empiriocriticism, the thesis explores whether his philosophy of science can be coherently interpreted as an original, albeit historically independent, form of pragmatic thought.

The analysis is grounded in a reconstruction of pragmatism as developed by Charles S. Peirce and William James, with particular attention to the pragmatic maxim and to pragmatism understood as a method for clarifying concepts through their practical consequences. Within this framework, concepts are approached not as representations with intrinsic ontological value, but as tools whose meaning lies in their function of orienting inquiry and action. This provides the conceptual backdrop for evaluating Mach’s epistemological positions.

The core of the thesis examines Mach’s epistemology, primarily through Knowledge and Error, focusing on his anti-metaphysical methodology, his rejection of the rigid physical–psychical dualism, and his critique of the thing-in-itself. Mach’s notion of “elements” is analyzed as a methodological strategy aimed at grounding knowledge in experience without reifying abstract distinctions. Particular attention is devoted to Mach’s account of cognition as an adaptive process shaped by biological and psycho-physiological constraints, in which association, memory, abstraction, hypothesis formation, and error play a central role.

Against this background, the thesis argues that Mach’s conception of concepts, hypotheses, and scientific laws as adaptive and economical instruments closely parallels the pragmatist emphasis on consequences, usability, and practical orientation. The analysis highlights the role of error as a productive moment in the advancement of knowledge and interprets Mach’s principle of the economy of thought as a criterion of cognitive efficiency rather than of truth in a strong metaphysical sense.

In conclusion, the thesis maintains that Mach’s epistemology can be rigorously read as an original form of pragmatism, characterized by the notions of operativity and orientation. Rather than offering a metaphysical account of reality, Mach provides a functional understanding of scientific knowledge in which concepts are evaluated according to their capacity to organize experience, guide expectations, and adapt to the evolving demands of inquiry.
